The internet has become a corporate dystopia.

Centralized corporate services control most of our digital lives. We rely on these services for our day to day computing, yet we have little control over them. The servers that govern our digital lives belong to giant companies. They aren’t ours to begin with.

This is an urgent problem in a world that depends on always-on, always-connected cloud computing.

Let’s leave it behind. Let’s compute.

The reason our digital lives are controlled by large corporate services boils down to a very simple fact: the individual does not control the server side. There’s simply no practical way for an ordinary user to run a personal server. Urbit confronts this problem head on.

Urbit solves this problem by redesigning the server-side from scratch.

Urbit is a unified personal cloud computing platform layered over the existing internet. Your Urbit stores your data, runs your apps, and manages your devices. You own your Urbit cryptographically, like you would bitcoin, and it lives on a new decentralized network owned and governed by its users.

An Urbit world is one in which individuals own and control their digital lives, and can freely communicate, transact and build communities without relying on any centralized corporate service.

Urbit is simple, but there’s a lot to talk about.

Urbit is a big project. It doesn’t really fit in a single buzzword-heavy paragraph. So, we put together this primer.

What Urbit is

A complete system for self-soverign cloud computing.

Urbit is a piece of software that can run on any internet-connected, unix-based computer. It is made up of a virtual machine, a programming language, and an operating system — all designed and built from scratch. The Urbit OS includes a filesystem, build system, web server, secret storage, application model, terminal and network protocol—all in about 30K lines of code. (For comparison, WordPress is about 500K lines of code).

We call an Urbit a personal server. It’s everything an ordinary person needs to run their own network-connected virtual computer.

At the bottom, it's a personal blockchain.

At its core, your Urbit works like a personal blockchain. Its state is a simple, deterministic, frozen function of its event history. This function is called Nock.

A blockchain is a public ledger; your Urbit is a private log. Only you compute it. Its events are your personal input stream and its state is your personal archive.

This uniform foundation means that all the nodes on the Urbit network share the same model of computation. If Ethereum is decentralized global computer, Urbit is decentralized global computing protocol.

It’s not a stack — it’s an OS. A tightly integrated system for cloud computing.

We believe that the complexity of the underlying system determines the complexity of the user experience. We think simple user experiences are better, so we built a software stack that’s ruthlessly simple. We think computing shouldn’t be a chore, it should be fun.

Urbit is a completely sealed system. It runs on top of Unix and the internet, but the neither the Urbit programmer nor the Urbit user ever need to know anything about the supporting infrastructure. No need to configure an array of components.

All of Urbit, except Nock, can be updated over the air. Software patches and updates are synced directly to your Urbit. Both for core system components, and any apps you install. Maintenance isn’t something the user should ever worry about.

Most importantly, Urbit includes everything needed for personal cloud computing. As a developer, this means no more fussing over what components to put in your stack and getting them all to work together. The OS *is* the stack. As a user, this means having apps that can all share resources — keeping all your computing in one system that you own and control.

On a new decentralized network where address space is cryptographic property.

Urbit runs its own networking protocol where each node is owned by a private key. New nodes on the network are born when their initial keys are signed by an existing supernode.

The initial 256 supernodes, called galaxies, are coded into the kernel itself. Each of these galaxies can initialize 256 stars. In turn, each of these 216 stars can initialize 216 planets — making for ~4B total.

Galaxies and stars are network infrastructure, planets are for individual humans, and moons are for devices.

This structure exists so that Urbit can grow toward decentralization. Early adopters are like homesteaders — they manage larger blocks in the form of supernodes (stars and galaxies) and participate in network governance.

Supernodes provide routing — connecting peers on the network. Once connected, peers talk directly. Children are never trapped under a parent; stars and planets can always move.

What Urbit is for

We have personal computers that fit on our desks, and personal computers that fit in our pockets — but personal computers that live on the network have never been fully realized.

Urbit is infrastructure for a world where computing is both ubiquitous and in the hands of those who rely on it. Urbit is for things we haven’t yet imagined — but we have thought about a few of them.

A single, unified interface for cloud computing.

Since the apps we use today don’t share a computing platform, they’re completely separate from one another. The data they store is siloed, they each require their own login, and present their own interface.

Your Urbit is designed to be a single, unified interface to all of your communication and collaboration. Sort of like WeChat, but completely open source and extensible (and of course not centrally controlled). One place for you to manage all of your data and communication that’s universally accessible.

A trusted platform for off-chain computation.

Blockchains are not well suited for everyday personal computing. They are slow, their transactions are public, and they can store only limited amounts of data. Furthermore, to interact with a blockchain, you need more than just a private key — you need a computer. Today, that computer is either run by a centralized corporate service, or it’s your laptop.

We believe that for blockchains to realize their full potential, individuals need to be able to interact with them directly. They need a trusted place for decentralized, off-chain computation that’s always online.

Your Urbit can hold your private keys and let you call out to the blockchain directly. No need to use an exchange or centralized corporate service. Plus, your Urbit is an always-on cloud computer. You can throw your laptop in the lake and not lose anything. Your Urbit holds all your personal data, and is much faster than a blockchain.

A decentralized internet of things

Your Urbit planet has addresses for 232 (~4B) moons. Your moons are for your devices. Your lights, your fridge, your 3d printer, your army of autonomous farming robots. Moons share a network and a filesystem with your planet. They can seamlessly sync with one another and share software.

Today, our ‘internet of things’ platforms are as good as our apps. They’re insecure, can’t interoperate with each other and rely on centralized corporate servers. We look forward to a world where it’s easy for computing to become diffuse and ubiquitous. Where our many devices can easily share a computing platform.

A permanent personal archive

Humanity has come to rely on technology to store the cultural record, but the longevity of our data remains entirely in the hands of others. Services can disappear, and our data along with them. Accounts can be censored or shut down unexpectedly.

Your urbit can capture every message you’ve ever sent, and every document you’ve ever edited, all your biometric data, even your genome. It can collect as much or as little data about you as you want, all in a single permanent personal archive.

Your Urbit is designed to store your data permanently, reliably, forever. We believe that our data should be accessible over the course of our entire lives, never vulnerable to a third party with the power to delete or edit it.

Development history

Urbit started as an open-ended personal project in 2002 concerned with the question: ‘how should we compute?’

The answer, in the abstract, is simple: a computer could be a simple device whose state is just a number, its update function is fixed, and all of its inputs are events. So, with a bit of time on his hands, Curtis went down to his garage, put on some Brian Eno, and set to work.

The first four years were spent inventing and designing all 33 lines of Nock. Nock is pretty tough to write, and not all that useful on its own.

So, it was time to build a programming language. Another four years went into writing Hoon as a strict, typed and self-compiling language with no reserved words.

But a programming language alone isn’t all that useful. So, three more years were spent imagining an OS for a computer that was intended to live permanently on the network.

After 11 years of being one man project, Urbit was able to boot its own OS.

After that, in 2013, we raised some seed funding to build a real, working alpha. We have been working quietly for the past few years to make Urbit as real and stable as possible.

Urbit hosts its own website, forum and chat. It has been running a live test network since 2013. Our standards are incredibly high — so we only see a lot of work to do. But the system is in good shape. It’s stable, usable and has a healthy community.
